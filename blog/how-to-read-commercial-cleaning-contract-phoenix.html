How to Read a Commercial Cleaning Contract (Without a Lawyer)

Published July 14, 2026 — CleanWorks Solutions

A commercial cleaning contract does not need to be complicated, but it does need to be clear. Most problems between a business and a cleaning company start with vague language: “general cleaning,” “as needed,” “periodic detail,” or a price that looks low because important tasks are missing.

For Phoenix offices, clinics, schools, and professional buildings, the contract should explain exactly what is cleaned, how often it is cleaned, what is excluded, and how either side can change or end the agreement. You do not need to be a lawyer to spot the major issues. You just need to slow down and read the agreement like a scope of work, not a sales brochure.

Start With the Scope, Not the Price

The monthly price only means something if the scope is specific. A cheaper quote may leave out floor machine work, interior glass, high dusting, supply restocking, or restroom detail. Another quote may cost more because it includes those tasks on a rotation.

Look for a list that separates daily, weekly, monthly, and occasional tasks. A useful contract should say things like:

  • Trash removed and liners replaced each visit
  • Restrooms cleaned and disinfected each visit
  • Hard floors dust mopped and damp mopped on scheduled service days
  • Breakroom counters, sinks, and tables cleaned each visit
  • High dusting, vent dusting, or baseboard detail handled monthly or quarterly if included

Be careful with phrases such as “as needed” unless the contract explains who decides when it is needed. If the customer and cleaner define that phrase differently, the disagreement usually shows up after the first month.

Check the Frequency and Access Details

The contract should state how many visits happen each week or month and when service will normally occur. It should also cover access: keys, alarm codes, lock-up responsibilities, holiday schedules, and what happens if the crew cannot enter the building.

This matters in Phoenix because many businesses prefer after-hours cleaning to avoid disrupting patients, clients, or staff. After-hours service can work well, but only if the contract explains entry procedures, alarm instructions, and emergency contact information.

Read the Price Increase Clause

Some contracts allow automatic price increases after a short period. Others allow increases if square footage changes, service frequency changes, labor costs rise, or supplies are added. None of that is automatically unreasonable, but it should be written plainly.

Ask these questions before signing:

  • Can the price increase during the first year?
  • How much notice is required before a price change?
  • Are supply costs included or billed separately?
  • Does the price change if your team adds rooms, expands hours, or increases traffic?

A fair contract does not hide price changes. It explains when they can happen and gives the customer time to review the new terms.

Understand Cancellation and Renewal Terms

Cancellation language is one of the most important parts of the agreement. Some contracts renew automatically for another full term unless you cancel during a narrow window. Others require 30 days written notice. Some include early termination fees.

Contract TermWhat to Look For
Cancellation noticeHow many days are required and whether email is acceptable
Automatic renewalWhether the contract renews monthly, annually, or for another fixed term
Performance issuesWhether the cleaner has a chance to fix problems before cancellation
Final invoiceHow final service dates, deposits, or unpaid balances are handled

A reasonable agreement gives both sides a clear exit path. If the contract makes it difficult to leave even when service is poor, that is worth addressing before you sign.

Confirm Insurance, Supplies, and Exclusions

The agreement should identify whether the cleaning company carries general liability insurance and workers' compensation where applicable. It should also explain who provides consumable supplies such as paper towels, toilet tissue, trash liners, hand soap, and sanitizer.

Exclusions are just as important as inclusions. Common exclusions include biohazard cleanup, exterior windows, carpet extraction, floor stripping and waxing, post-construction cleanup, pest-related cleaning, and moving heavy furniture. Those services may be available, but they should be quoted separately instead of assumed.

The Bottom Line

A good commercial cleaning contract should make service easier to manage, not harder. Read for scope, frequency, price changes, cancellation terms, insurance, supplies, and exclusions. If something is vague, ask for it to be rewritten before service starts.

The best contracts are boring in a good way: clear tasks, clear price, clear schedule, and clear expectations. That protects the business owner and the cleaning crew, and it gives everyone a better chance of maintaining the building consistently.
